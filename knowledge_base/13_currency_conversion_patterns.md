# Currency Conversion Patterns

## Overview

Currency conversion occurs when funds are paid in one currency and received in another. It is normal in international commerce but can raise AML risk with unclear purpose, repeated movement, or unusual differences.

## Why It Matters

Currency conversion can obscure the original value trail, especially when funds pass through several banks or jurisdictions. It may complicate comparison between paid and received amounts.

## Typical Indicators

- Repeated cross-currency transactions without clear purpose.
- Amount paid and amount received differ significantly.
- Currency conversion followed by rapid onward transfer.
- Conversion involving high-risk jurisdictions or unknown counterparties.
- Customer profile does not support foreign exchange activity.

## Analyst Considerations

Check whether foreign exchange activity is expected. Review purpose, counterparty relationship, amount difference, fees, exchange rate reasonableness, and onward movement.

## Example Scenario

A customer repeatedly pays in one currency and transfers in another with no declared international business need.

## Suggested Action

Escalate when cross-currency activity is combined with high value, cross-border movement, rapid transfers, or unexplained amount discrepancies.
