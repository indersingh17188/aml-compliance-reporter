# Structuring and Smurfing

## Overview

Structuring, also called smurfing, refers to breaking a larger value movement into several smaller transactions to avoid internal monitoring rules, reporting thresholds, or analyst review.

## Why It Matters

Structuring is suspicious because the pattern may indicate intent to avoid detection. A single transaction may look normal, but repeated timing, amount similarity, and relationships between accounts can reveal a hidden laundering pattern.

## Typical Indicators

- Multiple transactions just below a review threshold.
- Several transfers within a short time window.
- Similar values repeated across accounts.
- Activity inconsistent with the customer profile.
- Multiple senders or receivers connected by timing or amount.

## Analyst Considerations

Review recent transaction history, compare activity with expected customer behaviour, and check whether values cluster below monitoring limits. Repetition and timing are often more important than one amount.

## Example Scenario

A customer sends 12 transfers of similar value within two days, each below an internal review threshold, using the same payment format during unusual hours.

## Suggested Action

Escalate if behaviour is repeated or inconsistent with the customer profile. Enhanced due diligence may be required when structuring is combined with cross-border movement, multiple accounts, or unclear source of funds.
