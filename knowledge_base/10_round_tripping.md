# Round-Tripping

## Overview

Round-tripping occurs when funds leave an account or entity and later return through another route, creating the appearance of legitimate revenue, investment, or business activity.

## Why It Matters

Circular movement can disguise origin of funds, inflate activity, or create false documentation, and may support layering or integration.

## Typical Indicators

- Funds return to original sender or related party.
- Circular transfers between related companies or accounts.
- Similar amounts moving in opposite directions.
- Transactions lack economic purpose.
- Use of cross-border routes, currency conversion, or shell entities.

## Analyst Considerations

Review account relationships, timing, amount similarity, business rationale, ownership links, and whether transactions create artificial revenue.

## Example Scenario

Company A sends funds to Company B overseas. A similar amount returns weeks later from Company C, which shares ownership with Company B.

## Suggested Action

Escalate when circular movement lacks clear commercial purpose or involves related parties, high-risk jurisdictions, or opaque ownership.
