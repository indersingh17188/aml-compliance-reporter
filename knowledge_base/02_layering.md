# Layering

## Overview

Layering is the stage of money laundering where funds move through multiple transactions, accounts, institutions, currencies, or jurisdictions to obscure their origin.

## Why It Matters

Layering often involves complexity without a clear business purpose. Funds may move quickly between banks, countries, or currencies, making the trail harder to reconstruct.

## Typical Indicators

- Rapid movement of funds through several accounts or banks.
- Cross-border transfers without an obvious explanation.
- Currency conversion followed by additional transfers.
- Funds received and quickly paid onward.
- Circular or back-and-forth movement between related parties.

## Analyst Considerations

Review whether the transaction is part of a chain. Check previous and subsequent movements, customer profile, counterparty relationship, and economic purpose.

## Example Scenario

Funds are received in one currency, converted into another, and then transferred to a different bank shortly after receipt, without a known business need.

## Suggested Action

Escalate when layering indicators appear with high-value transfers, cross-currency movement, high-risk jurisdictions, or unknown counterparties.
