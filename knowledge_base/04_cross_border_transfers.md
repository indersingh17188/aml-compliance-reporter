# Cross-Border Transfers

## Overview

Cross-border transfers involve funds moving between countries or jurisdictions. They are common in legitimate finance but can increase risk when combined with unusual counterparties, high-risk jurisdictions, unclear purpose, or rapid onward movement.

## Why It Matters

International transfers can make it harder to verify source of funds, beneficial ownership, and transaction purpose. Jurisdictions may also have different secrecy or regulatory risks.

## Typical Indicators

- Transfer to or from a high-risk jurisdiction.
- No clear business or personal rationale for the country involved.
- Funds moved onward quickly after receipt.
- Cross-border movement combined with currency conversion.
- Frequent international transfers inconsistent with profile.

## Analyst Considerations

Check expected geographic activity, transaction purpose, counterparty relationship, and supporting documentation. Assess context rather than treating all international transfers as suspicious.

## Example Scenario

An individual with no declared international business sends repeated high-value transfers to a foreign corporate account.

## Suggested Action

Apply enhanced review when cross-border movement is combined with high value, high-risk geography, unclear purpose, or SHAP factors such as cross-currency and cross-bank risk.
